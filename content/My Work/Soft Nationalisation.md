This blogpost will argue that the existence of privatised AI labs substantially increases AI x-risk and that corporate incentives pushes us towards misalignment. This blogpost serves as the first in a series that advocates for "soft nationalisation."

The current incentive structures present in private AI labs actively encourage increasingly risky and unsafe behaviour as AI capabilities increase. I predict that most AI labs will go public in the future or enter partnerships with public companies, which introduces a fiduciary duty to shareholders. Going public is extremely bad for AI safety and increases p(misalignment). 

1. **The VC funding lifecycle** 

Most AI labs rely on [venture capital ](**[https://hbr.org/1998/11/how-venture-capital-works](https://hbr.org/1998/11/how-venture-capital-works)**)and/or private equity funding. VCs typically invest in dozens of AI companies with the goal of achieving a return on their overall portfolio. A general partner (GP) invests in dozens of firms on behalf of limited partners (LPs), who typically demand a return on investment within a compressed 2- to 5-year timeframe. The VC firm charges ~2% of total capital as management fees from its LPs, and profits from successful investments, typically ~20% carried interest. 

This incentive structure compels VCs to seek out and capitalize on a limited number of high-risk, high-reward investments (often <5% of the total portfolio) with the potential for massive, "homerun" returns [(often 10x to 100x)](**[https://www.bipevergreenfunds.com/news/explainer-understanding-the-venture-capital-power-law](https://www.bipevergreenfunds.com/news/explainer-understanding-the-venture-capital-power-law)**). 

This funding structure is probably good in the short run for fostering good AI research, attracting top talent, and it clearly seems to be [working](**[https://www.openvc.app/investor-lists/ai-investors?page=1](https://www.openvc.app/investor-lists/ai-investors?page=1)**) [for VCs](**[https://www.vcsheet.com/sheet/deeptech-fund](https://www.vcsheet.com/sheet/deeptech-funds)**). However, the VC model is not sustainable for most AI labs because it is not designed for perpetual private ownership. It presupposes a defined lifespan for AI companies and necessitates a “liquidity event”. For successful companies, this looks like an IPO (which leads to the public market), or an acquisition by a large public company (see [Deepmind's acquisition](**[https://archive.nytimes.com/dealbook.nytimes.com/2014/01/27/google-acquires-british-artificial-intelligence-developer/](https://archive.nytimes.com/dealbook.nytimes.com/2014/01/27/google-acquires-british-artificial-intelligence-developer/)**)). This is bad for successful companies for many (obvious) reasons, but most companies that are not immediately profitable end up disappearing in the pump-and-dump VC model (this operates under the [assumption](**[https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/](https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/)**) that the majority of small AI startups will not have an [IIR](**[https://www.roundtable.eu/learn/evaluating-venture-capital-funds-irr-dpi-tvpi-and-multiple](https://www.roundtable.eu/learn/evaluating-venture-capital-funds-irr-dpi-tvpi-and-multiple)**) of >15%).

This lifecycle creates intense pressure for the vast majority of AI companies to achieve profitability quickly. The goal is not merely to be profitable, but to deliver 10x to 100x returns in a compressed 2-5 year timeline or risk closure. AI companies already face immense pressure amid a highly competitive market, which leads to them prioritising speed and market dominance over AI safety (the “move fast and break things” mentality).  

**I see this as a significant source of AI risk in the form of deprioritising alignment and ethical research, building unsafe AI, and leading to norms that [lead to unsafe AI](**[https://www.cnbc.com/2024/10/24/openai-miles-brundage-agi-readiness.html](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/10/24/openai-miles-brundage-agi-readiness.html)**)**, not to mention the shareholder liability that comes with being a public company after making it out of the VC pipeline.

Perhaps an argument against this claim is that AGI/transformative AI is unlikely to be built in a small AI startup and that big AI labs don’t feel this financial pressure because everyone wants to invest in them.

However, I don’t think this is a particularly strong argument, because big labs are also heavily funded by VCs. Presumably, they feel a pressure to make profit and investors are highly concerned about making a ROI on their money. Additionally, the existence of smaller, more nimble companies creates an incentive for a “race to the bottom” on safety, even for big labs. While it is less probable for a small startup to create AGI, a highly capable open-source group or a rogue startup could plausible make a breakthrough and deploy capable models without sufficient testing or research. This puts pressure on big labs to cut corners on safety to avoid losing a lead. 

I think a slightly stronger argument is that we might want to accelerate capabilities in the short term and sacrifice a bit of safety to beat China, especially during a period of mass brain drain and poor foreign relations. The US is losing talent and [promising researchers](**[https://www.scmp.com/news/china/science/article/3323325/will-you-leave-us-china-it-depends-says-mathematician-terence-tao](https://www.scmp.com/news/china/science/article/3323325/will-you-leave-us-china-it-depends-says-mathematician-terence-tao)**), which on top of [new H1B visa policies](**[https://www.reuters.com/business/media-telecom/trump-mulls-adding-new-100000-fee-h-1b-visas-bloomberg-news-reports-2025-09-19/](https://www.reuters.com/business/media-telecom/trump-mulls-adding-new-100000-fee-h-1b-visas-bloomberg-news-reports-2025-09-19/)**) and anti-democratic university reforms, could potentially be a risk for the AI arms race. 

Some speculations: I anticipate that VCs funding sources will [slowly dry up](**[https://fortune.com/2025/09/19/zuckerberg-ai-bubble-definitely-possibility-sam-altman-collapse/](https://fortune.com/2025/09/19/zuckerberg-ai-bubble-definitely-possibility-sam-altman-collapse/)**) (in the next[ 2-3 years](**[https://finance.yahoo.com/news/jerome-powell-signs-ai-bubble-222907233.html](https://finance.yahoo.com/news/jerome-powell-signs-ai-bubble-222907233.html)**), according to my intuition).

A lot of AI startups are [unprofitable and fail quickly](**[https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/a](https://fortune.com/2025/08/18/mit-report-95-percent-generative-ai-pilots-at-companies-failing-cfo/a)**), although this is not necessarily a threat to the VC model unless consumers realise that as well. VCs are also finding out that AI startup decks with proof of concepts don’t translate well into [a final product](**[https://www.inc.com/joe-procopio/ai-hype-fueled-startups-are-starting-to-unravel/91235962](https://www.inc.com/joe-procopio/ai-hype-fueled-startups-are-starting-to-unravel/91235962)**). I think VCs will focus less on companies that directly work on AI capabilities and tools, but on industries that surround AI integration (see [YC’s latest calls for startups](**[https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs](https://www.ycombinator.com/rfs)**)). 

Open weight models are also becoming extremely impressive (gpt-oss, gemma, llama, Qwen perform [surprisingly well](**[https://simonwillison.net/2025/Jul/30/chinese-models/](https://simonwillison.net/2025/Jul/30/chinese-models/)**)), GPUs/compute is [extremely cheap](**[https://vast.ai/pricing](https://vast.ai/pricing)**) (an RTX 4090 is currently priced at $0.31/hour), and LLMs are free to use. I expect good and capable AI technology to move towards becoming non-excludable public goods in the next few years considering how cheap tokens and compute is.

Two possible outcomes:
- Labs start charging more for model usage. 
- Labs find a more sustainable funding method. 

2. **Big labs aren’t generating revenue and want more profit**

I am generally skeptical about AI safety under private labs (perhaps excluding labs with a heavy emphasis on AI safety, like Anthropic). Labs are not generating enough revenue from their pricing structure to sustainably fund their own operations, despite the hype and demand for AI. No AI lab made over $100 million by selling access to their own models. At the same time, labs are seeing an increased demand for their product, higher operational and R&D costs, and generally want to make more money. 

I don’t believe that labs will start increasing their prices for usage, since increasing prices in what is essentially an oligopolistic, highly competitive market comes at a huge cost to market share. Instead, I predict that more companies will go public because they would profit greatly from going public (see later discussion on MSFT after the OpenAI partnership). 

Case study: [OpenAI’s restructuring](**[https://openai.com/our-structure/](https://openai.com/our-structure/)**)

[OpenAI’s restructuring](**[https://www.openaifiles.org/restructuring](https://www.openaifiles.org/restructuring)**) and [MOU with Microsoft](**[https://openai.com/index/joint-statement-from-openai-and-microsoft/](https://openai.com/index/joint-statement-from-openai-and-microsoft/)**) is a major step toward prioritising private corporate interests over AI safety, despite having AGI explicitly carved out in the agreement. OpenAI underwent a restructuring in May and recently signed a non-binding memorandum of understanding with Microsoft. The restructuring moved the capped-profit for-profit arm under a [(no longer profit-capped) Public Benefit Corporation](**[https://openai.com/index/statement-on-openai-nonprofit-and-pbc/](https://openai.com/index/statement-on-openai-nonprofit-and-pbc/)**) (PBC). OpenAI and Microsoft’s partnership is governed by an agreement that allows Microsoft continued access to OpenAI’s technology.

From [https://notforprivategain.org/](https://notforprivategain.org/)

This move was explicitly designed to attract VC and private investors, and it aligns the financial interests of OpenAI with its stock equity. OpenAI’s removal of profit caps, means that investors, including AI researchers at OpenAI, Sam Altman, and Microsoft, [profit from increased research in capabilities](**[https://pitchbook.com/news/articles/openai-cancel-for-profit-transition-softbank](https://pitchbook.com/news/articles/openai-cancel-for-profit-transition-softbank)**). The OpenAI non-profit entity retains only a ~20% equity stake in the PBC, which means the safety-concerned aspect of the company has much less power during board votes than expected.

OpenAI is now intertwined in its partnership with Microsoft, a publicly-traded company with a minority interest in OpenAI. Hint: Microsoft stock has shot up by 30% since the OpenAI restructuring deal and currently has a 38 P/E with an industry average of 35x. Microsoft is likely looking for an ever higher equity stake in OAI in the future. 

At the same time, OpenAI has [consistently lobbied against AI safety laws](**https://ailabwatch.org/resources/company-advocacy**) like SB1047 and will likely continue to be anti-regulation. It has [disbanded their safety team](**[https://www.cnbc.com/2024/10/24/openai-miles-brundage-agi-readiness.html](https://www.cnbc.com/2024/10/24/openai-miles-brundage-agi-readiness.html)**), [failed to protect whistleblowers](**[https://static.politico.com/ed/a3/b49946554f5ead081a5df2063048/letter-from-openai-whistleblowers-on-sb-1047-2024-08-22-2.pdf](https://static.politico.com/ed/a3/b49946554f5ead081a5df2063048/letter-from-openai-whistleblowers-on-sb-1047-2024-08-22-2.pdf)**), and recently [subpoenaed the Midas Project](**[https://sfstandard.com/2025/09/03/openai-midas-project-elon-musk-subpoena/](https://sfstandard.com/2025/09/03/openai-midas-project-elon-musk-subpoena/)**), an AI safety watchdog organisation, for their reporting.

I am concerned that OpenAI’s new direction is indicative of the path AI labs will go down under the private model and without substantial government intervention. I’m worried that OpenAI’s restructuring and integration with Microsoft leads to even more misaligned incentives and pushes them towards capabilities research even further than [they already are](**[https://www.wired.com/story/open-ai-nonprofit-transition-activism/a](https://www.wired.com/story/open-ai-nonprofit-transition-activism/a)**).

3.  **AGI-pilled leaders and mutually assured destruction**

I think some people are more optimistic about private AI companies developing AGI because they think AI lab CEOs and leaders are sufficiently AGI pilled and care about alignment as much as us. Presumably, this is because if anyone builds it, everyone dies (haha). I think that this is just untrue. Mutually assured destruction and AGI-pilled AI leaders will not prevent AI labs from releasing and creating unsafe models, or deploying uncontrolled transformative AI. 

First, MAD primarily works in the situation where both parties have access to the weapons and where the deployment of the weapon will cause equal harm to both parties. This is defined in a very narrow sense - obviously countries are deterred by international laws and norms, etc, but in the AGI case it seems very possible that a lab can build misaligned and unsafe but controllable AGI. E.g. a lab could have complete control and ownership over a model’s deployment protocol, weights, and algorithm, and the model could be wildly unsafe and do dangerous things with the caveat of it not harming the creator. 

Without having to explicitly model it, the race dynamics in the AI arms race are much more unstable than the nuclear arms race. Arguably, the stakes are higher. An American AI lab CEO is simultaneously competing with other western AI labs to build more capable models while also racing with China. 

Second of all, I think that we inaccurately model the behaviour and incentives of these leaders and paint them to be more rational than they are. We tend to see AI lab leaders as “smart AI safety guys” when in reality AI labs (except maybe Anthropic) have been [consistently lobbying against AI safety regulation](**https://ailabwatch.org/resources/company-advocacy**). It seems likely to me that AI leaders can also be profit-driven, arrogant, and miscalibrated, and might make the wrong choices when faced with extremely high stakes and potentially trillions in profit. I think we should be less optimistic about the “we’ll be the responsible ones” justification to try to race to AGI for AI leaders who are simultaneously attracted to the profit that it brings and are virtually unrestrained. 

**Conclusion**

I think the main takeaways should be:
- The VC funding model is dangerous for AI safety
- More AI labs will go public in the next 1-3 years
- We shouldn’t trust AI-pilled leaders

This is not to say that nationalised AI labs have better incentives. Hard nationalisation seems even more flawed and dangerous to me, since it risks us losing the AI race. I will probably write another blogpost about the nationalisation and how it unevenly distributes funding burden onto tax payers. 

Note: I think there is a minor point to be made about self-selection and the type of people who tend to run VC-backed AI startups. My guess is that more of them are less concerned about AI safety, care less about social impact, and more profit-driven than the people doing the same type of work which is funded by grants or academia. However, this is a very weak, intuition-based claim and I’m not able to back that up.
